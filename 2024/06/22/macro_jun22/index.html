Markets Recap - Advances in US and EU; Declines in Japan and China

Summary

  • Despite different driving factors, both US and EU markets advanced this week, with the US market buoyed by strong business activity data and potential rate cuts, and the EU market lifted by easing political uncertainties and improving inflation outlooks.

  • Japanese and Chinese stock markets both ended lower this week, with Japan impacted by BoJ-related uncertainty despite positive export data, and China affected by weaker-than-expected economic indicators despite government support.

  • The US dollar strengthened on robust economic data amid divergent global central bank policies, WTI crude futures rose due to supply concerns and geopolitical tensions, while gold retreated slightly despite ongoing geopolitical uncertainties.


US Market :

The US stock market advanced this week, with the S&P 500 Index achieving fresh all-time highs and posting a 0.61% gain. The week began quietly due to a disappointing reading on declining savings, which raised concerns about future consumer spending and retail sales. Notably, sales at bars and restaurants fell by 0.4%, indicating reduced discretionary spending. However, the market rebounded later in the week due to a stronger-than-expected reading on business activity, which exceeded consensus expectations.


The bond market also reacted positively to the robust business activity data. The US 10-year Treasury yield moved lower, yet the overall bond market performance was bolstered by encouraging comments from Federal Reserve officials about the potential for a rate cut later this year.


Economic Data

  • Retail sales increased by only 0.1% in May according to advance estimates, following a downwardly revised 0.2% decline in April.

  • The Federal Reserve announced that industrial production expanded by 0.9% in May, significantly above consensus expectations and marking the fastest pace in nearly a year. Factories operated at 78.7% capacity, slightly above expectations and the highest level since last November.

  • S&P Global reported that its composite index of business activity rose to 54.6 in June, indicating strong economic momentum.

EU Market :

EU stock markets advanced this week as concerns about political uncertainty eased and the outlook for monetary policy easing improved. Major stock indexes recorded gains, with Germany’s DAX increasing by 0.90%, France’s CAC 40 Index rising by 1.67%, and Italy’s FTSE MIB climbing by 1.97%. The UK’s FTSE 100 Index added 1.12%.


The bond market also responded positively, with long-term yields declining due to less persistent inflation readings and signals from central banks. The Bank of England (BoE) left its key interest rate unchanged at a 16-year high of 5.25%. However, some members of the Monetary Policy Committee indicated that the decision was finely balanced, suggesting that policymakers might be moving closer to reducing borrowing costs later this year. Notably, the headline inflation rate dropped to the central bank’s target of 2% in May, down from 2.3% the previous month.


Economic Data

  • UK’s flash composite PMI falls in June, missing consensus

  • Germany’s manufacturing and service activities ease in June

  • France flash PMI readings show easing manufacturing and services activity in June

  • UK’s retail sales rise 2.9% in May

  • EU passenger car registrations drop 3% in May

  • UK’s inflation rate back to 2% target in May

  • Euro area inflation rate accelerates to 2.6% in May as expected

Asian Market :

The Japanese stock market ended lower this week due to uncertainty surrounding the Bank of Japan (BoJ), despite positive inflation data. Economic indicators were mixed, with Japan’s exports surging 13.5% year-over-year in May, supported by a weaker yen and strong shipments to the U.S. and China. However, this was offset by weaker PMI data. The yield on the 10-year Japanese government bond (JGB) rose to 0.97% from 0.93% at the end of the previous week. Investors were reacting to data showing accelerated inflation in May and speculating on how this might influence the BoJ’s decision on interest rates. BoJ Governor Kazuo Ueda reiterated that a rate hike in July is possible, depending on incoming data, while the central bank is also set to detail its planned tapering of JGB purchases in the same month.


Chinese stock markets retreated as economic data dampened investor sentiment, despite government support. The Shanghai Composite Index fell by 1.14%, and the blue-chip CSI 300 declined by 1.3%. Industrial production rose by a weaker-than-expected 5.6% in May year-over-year, down from April’s 6.7%. Fixed asset investment grew by 4% from January to May compared to the same period the previous year, but this marked a slowdown from the growth seen between January and April, with real estate investment declines deepening.


Economic Data :

  • Japan’s June factory activity growth unexpectedly eases; services PMI shrinks

  • Japan’s inflation rate accelerates in May to 2.8%; core inflation lower than expected at 2.5%

  • PBOC keeps one-year, five-year loan prime rates unchanged

  • Japan’s exports grow more than expected in May amid weak yen and robust external demand

  • Trade conflict: China probe on EU pork imports seen as response to EV tariffs

  • China property investment continues to decline despite support measures

  • China industrial production slows in May, retail sales top estimates

  • China’s new home prices fell 0.7% in May, accelerating from a 0.6% drop in April, marking the steepest month-on-month contraction in nearly a decade, according to the statistics bureau.

Other Markets :

The US dollar strengthened this week, with the Dollar Index surging above 105.8 on Friday, reaching a seven-week high. This increase was driven by robust S&P PMI data, which heightened expectations that the Federal Reserve may delay interest rate cuts compared to other major central banks. In contrast, the European Central Bank (ECB), Swiss National Bank (SNB), and Bank of Canada (BOC) have already initiated rate cuts. The Bank of England (BoE) is also preparing to reduce borrowing costs soon, while the Bank of Japan (BOJ) is anticipated to raise rates.


WTI crude futures climbed to 80.73 per barrel on Friday due to concerns over global oil demand-supply dynamics and escalating conflicts in the Middle East. Despite these tensions, Thursday’s data revealed a decline in US crude stockpiles by 2.547 million barrels last week, surpassing expectations of a 2 million barrel drawdown.


Gold experienced a decline of 0.44% for the week but remained near two-week highs. The retreat was influenced by clearer expectations regarding central bank decisions and ongoing geopolitical tensions, particularly in Gaza. On Thursday, reports indicated that multiple Israeli airstrikes in the Gaza Strip resulted in the deaths of at least 14 people and left dozens more injured.
