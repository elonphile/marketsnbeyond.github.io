Markets Recap - ECB Leads, US Labor Market Strength, Asian Markets Show Mixed Results

Summary

  • Despite mixed economic data, both US and EU stock markets experienced gains, driven by central bank decisions and sector-specific growth. Bond markets in both regions responded positively to easing inflation and interest rate cuts.

  • Asian markets displayed mixed performance, with Japanese stocks remaining relatively stable amidst currency challenges and positive sectoral growth indicators, while Chinese stocks faced declines despite some positive economic signals, indicating continued volatility and uncertainty in the region.

  • The currency markets saw the US dollar appreciate against major currencies, driven by speculation about the Federal Reserve’s policy stance. In commodity markets, WTI crude futures declined amidst concerns over borrowing costs and a potential supply surplus, while gold prices fell further due to expectations of interest rate cuts and declining volatility.


US Market :

The US stock markets ended higher this week, despite contradictory economic data. The S&P 500 Index rose by 1.32% and the technology-heavy Nasdaq Composite increased by 2.52%. The performance was bolstered by the continued enthusiasm for the artificial intelligence (AI) sector, even though the momentum in this fast-growing sector seemed to wane slightly.


The US bond market reacted positively to easing inflation data this week. Longer-term bond yields fell by 6%, reflecting investor confidence. Inflation data indicated that overall inflation was concentrated in the service sector, while the manufacturing sector saw some relief due to falling commodity prices.


Economic Data

  • PMI Mixed results this week. The Manufacturing PMI fell below 50, indicating contraction, with a reading of 48.7. In contrast, the Services PMI showed strength, jumping to 53.8, its highest level in nine months and well above consensus expectations.

  • The Labor Department reported that job openings in April fell to 8.059 million, the lowest level since February 2022.

  • Employers added 272,000 jobs in May, surpassing consensus expectations and marking the highest monthly increase since the beginning of the year.

  • The unemployment rate rose to 4.0%, the highest level since January 2022.

EU Market :

The EU stock market ended higher this week for most indices. The pan-European STOXX Europe 600 Index gained 1.04% after the European Central Bank (ECB) cut interest rates for the first time in five years on Thursday. Major stock indexes recorded gains: Italy’s FTSE MIB rose 0.49%, Germany’s DAX tacked on 0.32%, and France’s CAC 40 Index added 0.11%. The only exception was the UK’s FTSE 100 Index, which slipped 0.36%.


The EU bond market also reacted to the ECB decision, with most longer-term bond yields down this week. The ECB reduced its deposit rate by a quarter point to 3.75%, as expected, but stopped short of indicating that more cuts could follow. The ECB stated, “Based on an updated assessment of the inflation outlook, the dynamics of underlying inflation, and the strength of monetary policy transmission, it is now appropriate to moderate the degree of monetary policy restriction after nine months of holding rates steady.”


Economic Data

  • German factory orders fall unexpectedly in April

  • Germany’s composite PMI for May rises to 52.4, highest in a year

  • France services PMI falls to 49.3 in May, slightly missing initial estimate

  • Germany’s May manufacturing PMI rises to 45.40, highest in four months

Asian Market :

The Japanese stock markets remained relatively unchanged this week, with the Nikkei 225 posting a modest gain of 0.5%. A tentative rally in the yen, which strengthened to around JPY 155 against the U.S. dollar from the previous week’s JPY 157, posed a headwind for Japanese exporters. However, sentiment was bolstered by the latest purchasing managers’ index data, which showed that the country’s services sector continued to expand at a sharp pace in May. This indicates that housing may not be a drag on the economy for the first time in the past 14 months. In the bond market, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) was expected to taper bond buying to avoid a sharp tightening in financial conditions.


Stocks in China retreated this week despite positive data suggesting that the property sector may be gaining traction and encouraging PMI data. The Shanghai Composite Index declined by 1.15%, while the blue-chip CSI 300 Index edged down by 0.16%. New home sales slumped 33.6% in May from a year ago, though this was an improvement from April’s 45% decline. The private Caixin survey, which focuses on smaller and export-oriented firms, presented a more positive picture compared to official data from the previous week, which showed that manufacturing activity unexpectedly contracted in May.


Economic Data :

  • China’s trade surplus widens in May amid a bigger-than-expected exports surge

  • Japan services PMI revised upward to 53.8 in May amid a rise in new orders

  • China’s May services activity expands at fastest pace in ten months amid output growth

  • China’s manufacturing activity for May expands at fastest pace in nearly two years to 51.7

Other Markets :

The US dollar appreciated this week, fueled by a robust payrolls report that reignited speculation about the Federal Reserve potentially refraining from multiple interest rate cuts this year. This appreciation was particularly notable compared to the euro, especially after the European Central Bank’s decision to cut interest rates.


WTI crude futures declined by around 2% to $75.5 per barrel this week, marking the third consecutive week of losses. This decline was driven by increasing expectations that borrowing costs could remain elevated for an extended period, negatively impacting the demand outlook. A robust US jobs report in May supported a hawkish stance from the Federal Reserve. Concerns over a potential supply surplus were also heightened following the latest OPEC+ decision earlier in the week.


Gold extended earlier losses to trade below $2,320 per ounce on Friday, reaching its lowest level in a month. Traders anticipated central banks would cut interest rates soon, with declining volatility making gold, typically considered a safe haven, less attractive.
