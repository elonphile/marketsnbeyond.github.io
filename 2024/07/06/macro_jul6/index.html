Markets Recap - Fed Rate-Cut Expectations, Election Clarity, Asian Markets Mixed

Summary

  • This week, the US stock market rose as economic data and Powell’s comments led to a decline in the 10-year Treasury yield, which in turn provided support for higher stock prices by enhancing the appeal of equities due to rates cuts. While EU stock markets rose this week due to election clarity, while mixed economic data and a more hawkish ECB stance led to varied performance in the bond market.

  • Asian markets this week saw Japanese stocks rise on yen depreciation boosting exports, while Chinese markets faced declines amid disappointing manufacturing data indicating economic slowdown.

  • Global markets this week saw the dollar weakening amid Fed rate-cut expectations, bolstering gold prices, while crude oil climbed on strong demand and Gulf of Mexico supply concerns due to Hurricane Beryl.


US Market :

The US stock market ended higher, with the S&P 500 Index climbing to record highs, gaining 1.95%. This upward movement was driven by expectations of lower interest rates, which could favor future earnings by applying a lower implied discount. Investors anticipate an interest rate cut in September.


Bond markets reacted to Powell’s comments and the week’s economic data, leading to a decline in long-term U.S. Treasury yields. Cooling inflation data and a stable job market supported expectations for a soft landing. The ISM data indicated slowing growth in input prices for both service providers and manufacturers. Additionally, reassuring remarks from Fed Chair Jerome Powell at a European banking conference on Tuesday seemed to bolster investor confidence. While he noted that inflation was unlikely to reach the Fed’s 2.0% target until late 2025 or 2026, he acknowledged significant progress in reducing it.


Economic Data

  • On Monday, the Institute for Supply Management (ISM) reported its lowest reading of manufacturing activity (48.5) since February, with levels below 50.0 indicating contraction.

  • ISM’s gauge of current services sector activity, reported Wednesday, plunged five points into contraction territory, from 53.8 in May to 48.8 in June.

  • On Tuesday, the Labor Department’s JOLTS report revealed that job openings slightly increased to 8.14 million in May, up from a downwardly revised 7.9 million in April, the lowest level in over three years.

EU Market :

EU stock markets ended higher, buoyed by the clarity of election results. The STOXX Europe 600 Index gained 1.01%. Political uncertainty diminished as the far-right in France failed to secure an outright majority in the first round of legislative elections on June 30. Meanwhile, the Labour Party won the UK general election on July 4 with a significant majority. Major stock indexes rose, with France’s CAC 40 Index climbing 2.62%, Germany’s DAX gaining 1.32%, and Italy’s FTSE MIB increasing by 2.51%. The UK’s FTSE 100 Index added 0.49%.


However, the EU bond market showed mixed performance this week due to varied economic data and a more hawkish stance from the European Central Bank (ECB). At the ECB’s annual retreat in Portugal, President Christine Lagarde struck a slightly more hawkish tone, stating that Europe faces “several uncertainties regarding future inflation, particularly how the interaction of profits, wages, and productivity will evolve, and whether new supply-side shocks will impact the economy.” The final estimate of eurozone inflation confirmed that the year-over-year change in consumer prices decreased to 2.5% in June. In contrast, Germany’s manufacturing sector weakened further in May, with seasonally adjusted orders falling 1.6% sequentially and industrial production contracting by 2.5%.

Asian Market :

Japanese stock markets outperformed due to continued weakening of the yen, with the Nikkei 225 Index climbing 3.36%. A depreciating yen typically benefits export-focused industries. Japan’s 10-year sovereign bond yield rose to around 1.1%, its highest level since 2011, supported by expectations for actions from the Bank of Japan (BOJ). Speculation grew that a significantly weaker yen could prompt the BOJ to raise rates in July. The central bank highlighted that a weak yen increases import costs, contributing to inflationary pressures and impacting household consumption.


Chinese stock markets declined as disappointing manufacturing data raised concerns about economic slowdown. The Shanghai Composite Index recorded losses of 0.59%, while the blue-chip CSI 300 Index fell by 0.88%. China’s manufacturing sector contracted for the second consecutive month in June, with the official manufacturing Purchasing Managers’ Index (PMI) holding at 49.5, unchanged from May, amid declines in new orders and exports. The Caixin Services PMI was 51.2 in June, missing economists’ forecasts and slowing from 54 in May.


Economic Data :

  • Japan services PMI revised lower to 49.4 in June from preliminary data

  • China’s service activity growth softens to slowest pace in eight months

  • China’s manufacturing activity remains in contraction for second month at 49.5 in June

  • China’s factory growth in June reaches highest level in over three years to 51.8

  • Japan’s largest union group reported an average wage increase of 5.1%, the largest rise in 33 years

Other Markets :

The dollar index fell below 105 on Friday, a level not seen since mid-June, following the latest jobs report which reinforced expectations that the Fed will begin its rate-cutting cycle in September. Meanwhile, a more hawkish stance from the ECB contributed to the appreciation of the Euro against the Dollar. The Japanese yen also strengthened, crossing 161 per dollar, rebounding slightly from 38-year lows, primarily benefiting from a decline in the dollar.


WTI crude futures climbed 2.33% this week to $83.44 per barrel, driven by robust summer fuel demand and potential supply disruptions from hurricanes in the Gulf of Mexico. The US EIA reported a significant 12.2 million barrel drop in US crude stockpiles last week, far exceeding the anticipated 680K barrel draw. Additionally, Hurricane Beryl, a Category 2 storm, made landfall in Mexico, causing at least 11 fatalities and widespread damage across several Caribbean islands, further heightening supply concerns.


Gold ended higher this week with 2.77% gains for the week for the weakening dollar. The US jobs report reinforced expectations of upcoming rate cuts by the Federal Reserve, which pressured the dollar and triggered broad gains in precious metals.
