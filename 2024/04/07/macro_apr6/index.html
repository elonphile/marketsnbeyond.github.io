Markets Recap - Stocks Stall on Rate Cut Concerns, Asian Markets Diverge

Summary

  • Stock markets in both the US and EU retreated this week amid concerns over potential delays in interest rate cuts, fueled by hawkish comments from US Federal Reserve policymakers and a surge in crude oil prices.

  • Mixed performance in Asian markets this week, with Japanese stocks declining amid global uncertainties while Chinese equities outperformed on positive economic indicators and policy support.

  • Other markets saw the dollar strengthening on positive economic data, gold hitting a record high amid US rate cut expectations, and crude oil remaining steady amidst escalating geopolitical tensions.


US Market :

Stocks retreated from record highs as signs of a potential manufacturing sector revival raised concerns about inflationary pressures. The pullback was notable across large-cap indexes, with U.S. Treasury yields rising in response to the burgeoning optimism in the manufacturing sector. Energy stocks, however, bucked the trend, outperforming on the back of surging oil prices driven by geopolitical tensions between Israel and Iran, coupled with decisions by major exporters to maintain production limits amid tightening markets.


The 10-year U.S. Treasury note experienced a notable surge, reaching its highest intraday level since late November earlier in the week. Sentiment was heavily influenced by the Institute for Supply Management’s (ISM) reports, particularly the unexpected expansion in the manufacturing sector indicated by the ISM manufacturing reading on Monday. Of concern for potential interest rate cuts, the ISM prices paid index also exceeded expectations, suggesting a rebound in input prices, which could contribute to inflationary pressures.


Economic Data

  • The ISM manufacturing report, released Monday, surpassed expectations, indicating expansion for the first time in 16 months

  • Wednesday’s ISM services report provided some relief, easing worries amidst the broader economic landscape

  • Employers added a robust 303,000 jobs in March, significantly exceeding expectations and marking the strongest job growth in nearly a year

EU Market :

European stock markets retreated this week, influenced by hawkish comments from some U.S. Federal Reserve policymakers and heightened crude oil prices, which raised doubts about the timing of potential interest rate cuts. The pan-European STOXX Europe 600 Index declined by 0.40% over the holiday-shortened week, marking an end to 10 consecutive weeks of gains. Among major indices, France’s CAC 40 Index dropped by 0.85%, Germany’s DAX weakened by 0.08%, Italy’s FTSE MIB lost 1.16%, and the UK’s FTSE 100 Index declined marginally by 0.03%.


The bond markets also reacted to signals suggesting a potential delay in interest rate cuts by the US Federal Reserve, despite Eurozone inflation nearing its target and signs of economic revival. Annual inflation in the eurozone decelerated more than anticipated, slowing to 2.4% in March from 2.6% in February. Core inflation, excluding volatile food and energy prices, also moderated to 2.9% from 3.1%. However, evidence suggests a potential pickup in the economy after a period of stagnation. S&P Global revised its estimate for the eurozone’s composite purchasing managers’ index (PMI) to 50.3 in March from an initial 49.9, indicating possible improvement.


Economic Data

  • Euro Area retail sales drop 0.50% in February, more than expected

  • Industrial production in France rises 0.2% in February

  • Germany’s factory orders rise 0.2% in Feb

  • UK Services PMI drops to 53.1 in March

  • Eurozone Services PMI at nine-month high

  • Germany’s services PMI at 50.1, a six-month high

  • France services PMI nudges lower in March

  • Spain’s services PMI rises in March

  • Germany’s inflation rate drops to 2.2% in March, lowest since May 2021

  • UK’s manufacturing activity surges to 20-month high in March

  • Eurozone’s manufacturing activity declines to 3-month low

Asian Market :

The week saw Japan’s stock markets experiencing a decline, with the Nikkei 225 Index slumping by 2.83%. Heightened geopolitical tensions and uncertainty surrounding the U.S. Federal Reserve’s monetary policy trajectory weighed on global equities. In Japan, speculation persisted about potential intervention to support the yen, as the Japanese currency hovered around high-JPY 151 against the U.S. dollar, its lowest level in approximately 34 years. Meanwhile, the Bank of Japan (BoJ) hinted at the possibility of another interest rate hike, leading to a rise in the yield on the 10-year Japanese government bond to 0.77%.


Chinese stock markets outperformed this week, bolstered by the Qingming holiday and positive economic data indicating potential traction in the economy. March’s indicators reinforced hopes of an economic recovery, with the official manufacturing Purchasing Managers’ Index (PMI) surpassing expectations, reaching 50.8 in March compared to 49.1 in February. This expansion, driven by rebounds in production and exports, marked the first since September last year. The People’s Bank of China, in its first-quarter policy report, pledged to intensify existing measures to stimulate demand and maintain ample social financing and money supply to support Beijing’s annual growth target of 5%.


Economic Data :

  • China’s NBS manufacturing PMI returns to expansion; services growth at 9-month high

  • Japan’s Tokyo inflation slows as expected in March

  • Japan’s industrial output slips 0.1% in February, retail sales exceed forecasts

Other Markets :

The dollar index surged to 104.6 on Friday, buoyed by strong manufacturing data and a robust jobs report, fueling expectations of prolonged higher interest rates. In contrast, the Japanese yen depreciated to 151 per dollar, reaching its highest level in two weeks, as Japanese authorities escalated verbal interventions to stem sharp declines in the currency. Japanese Finance Minister Shunichi Suzuki reiterated warnings of potential government measures to support the yen.


WTI crude futures hovered near $87 per barrel on Friday, poised for a second consecutive week of gains. Escalating geopolitical tensions in oil-producing regions, OPEC+ efforts to curb supply, and a strong outlook for energy demand contributed to the rally in oil prices. Earlier in the week, Iran vowed retaliation against Israel for an attack on Iran’s embassy compound in Syria, resulting in the death of high-ranking Iranian personnel.


Gold prices soared past $2,320 an ounce on Friday, marking a new record high and extending gains for the third consecutive week. The precious metal benefited from US interest rate cut expectations, speculative buying, and central bank purchases amid robust US job growth in March. However, Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari’s remarks on Thursday hinted that if inflation remains high, rate cuts may not be necessary this year.
