Book Review - A Moverable Feast

Book info


The book details Hemingway’s first marriage to Hadley Richardson and his associations with other cultural figures of the Lost Generation in Interwar France, offering readers a glimpse into his formative years as a struggling writer in 1920s Paris. Published posthumously in 1964. This memoir captures Hemingway’s experiences and interactions with other literary luminaries of the time. Through its pages, readers are transported to the bohemian streets of Paris, where Hemingway navigates the challenges of artistic pursuit while rubbing shoulders with figures such as F. Scott Fitzgerald and Gertrude Stein.


Personal Takeaway


In ‘A Moveable Feast,’ Hemingway’s depiction of his modest yet joyous existence in Paris struck a chord with me. While I couldn’t fully engage with the café culture or grasp the nuances of the artistic milieu he encountered, his evocative portrayal of Parisian scenes, such as the blossoming horse chestnut trees in the Luxembourg Garden, transported me to familiar settings near my home. I found resonance in his descriptions of serene moments by the river and the enchanting pathways leading to Versailles, illustrating the timeless allure of Paris. Despite his financial constraints, Hemingway’s encounters with kindness in Paris echoed the warmth I experienced through my grandmother’s gestures in a foreign land. Moreover, I empathized with his acknowledgment of the city’s bleak seasons, reminiscent of my own less favorable times in Paris. Yet, even amidst its darkest days, Paris exuded a peculiar charm, reminiscent of snowy winters and mist-shrouded sunrises.


Through Hemingway’s narrative lens, I came to understand Paris not merely as a geographical entity but as a tapestry of diverse memories, each individual’s encounter unique yet interwoven. Though my memories of Paris remain etched in my heart, I am forever indebted to the richness it brought into my life. If you are lucky enough to have lived in Paris as a young man, then wherever you go for the rest of your life, it stays with you, for Paris is a moveable feast. Yes, I am so lucky.


But Paris was a very old city and we were young and nothing was simple there, not even poverty, nor sudden money, nor the moonlight, nor right and wrong nor the breathing of someone who lay beside you in the moonlight.
