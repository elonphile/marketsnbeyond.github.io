Markets Recap - Mixed Signals in US, Positive Sentiment in EU, and Currency Fluctuations Amidst Economic Uncertainty

Summary

  • Mixed performance in the US markets this week as investors reacted to Federal Reserve signals and company earnings, with hopes of potential rate cuts contrasting with concerns over mixed economic data, including an unexpected rise in the unemployment rate. While positive investor sentiment prevailed this week in EU, buoyed by the European Central Bank’s cautious stance and revisions to inflation forecasts.

  • Asian markets saw Japan’s Nikkei surpassing 40,000 for the first time amid historic yen weakness, and Chinese equities gained on government stabilization measures with surprising exports data.

  • Amid central bank signals and economic uncertainties, currency markets saw appreciation for the yen, euro, and GBP against the dollar, while WTI crude futures experienced a weekly decline on cautious sentiment about Chinese demand, and gold surged to a record high as a safe-haven asset amidst expectations of imminent interest rate cuts.


US Market :

Stock markets showed mixed performance as investors reacted to signals from the Federal Reserve and company earnings. Hopes of potential interest rate cuts by the Fed buoyed the S&P 500 Index and S&P MidCap 400 Index to new record intraday highs, along with the Nasdaq Composite, though they retreated slightly by the end of the week. Mega-cap tech shares, however, lagged behind, influenced in part by Apple’s decline due to reports of slowing iPhone sales in China. Notably, Novo Nordisk, a Danish pharmaceutical company, surpassed Tesla as the 12th largest public company by market capitalization. Mixed signals from the job report kept investor sentiment muted throughout the week.


Bond markets reflected positively in respnse to the Powell testimony. Fed Chair Jerome Powell’s testimony before Congress provided some insight into the timing of potential rate cuts. Powell’s remarks suggested a less hawkish stance, indicating that policymakers were nearing confidence in the sustained downtrend of inflation, potentially paving the way for rate cuts.


Economic Data

  • Friday’s jobs report initially reassured investors about the labor market, with employers adding more jobs than forecasted in February.

  • However, January’s gains were revised lower, and the unemployment rate unexpectedly rose to its highest level in over two years, reaching 3.9%.

EU Market :

In the EU stock markets, most indices ended the week on a positive note, except for the UK. Investor sentiment was buoyed by the European Central Bank’s (ECB) revised inflation forecasts, with notable gains seen in Italy’s FTSE MIB, France’s CAC 40 Index, and Germany’s DAX. Additionally, companies like Novo Nordisk stood out, displacing Tesla as the 12th biggest public company by market capitalization due to strong demand for its diabetes and weight loss drugs. However, the UK market faced some noise, particularly surrounding Jeremy Hunt’s announcement in his last Spring Budget before a general election. The reduction in national insurance rates and adjustments to tax statuses were notable factors affecting investor perceptions.


In the bond markets, ten-year government bond yields for Germany, Italy, and France declined following the ECB’s decision to leave its monetary policy unchanged. The central bank signaled the possibility of interest rate cuts in June and revised its inflation and economic growth forecasts lower. ECB President Christine Lagarde acknowledged progress toward the 2.0% inflation target but emphasized the need for sustained confidence in falling inflation. The central bank now anticipates inflation reaching 2.0% by 2025, a revision from the previous forecast of 2026.


Economic Data

  • ECB keeps rates unchanged for fourth meeting; revises down inflation projections, and ECB policymakers said to back first rate cut in June

  • Euro Area producer price inflation stays flat in January

  • UK services PMI marginally lower in February, composite PMI increases

  • Germany’s services PMI at 48.3 in February, a two-month high, France services PMI rises to 48.4 in February, beating consensus, while France industrial production drops 1.1% in January

  • Euro Area services and composite PMI rises in February

Asian Market :

Japan’s Nikkei surpassed the 40,000 mark for the first time, continuing its historic rally driven by significant yen weakness over the past three years. However, the index saw a slight dip from Thursday’s levels, attributed partly to decreased enthusiasm from tech companies. In the bond markets, investor speculation persisted regarding the Bank of Japan’s potential monetary policy trajectory, with growing conviction that the central bank may be nearing a decision to raise short-term interest rates from negative territory. Comments by a BoJ board member indicated optimism about a virtuous cycle of prices rising alongside wages.


Chinese equities saw gains as recent government measures to stabilize the market boosted investor confidence despite uncertainties in the economic outlook. The Shanghai Composite Index and the blue chip CSI 300 both posted modest increases. During the National People’s Congress (NPC), China’s parliament, Beijing set an economic growth target of around 5% for the year, consistent with the previous year’s target. In the bond market, the government announced plans to maintain a budget deficit target of around 3% and issued special ultra-long central government bonds worth RMB 1 trillion to support growth.


Economic Data :

  • China’s trade surplus larger than expected in February amid surge in exports, with above-consensus 7.1% in January and February from the prior-year period, improving from December’s 2.3% increase

  • China sets bold growth targets, calls for ‘support from all fronts’

  • China’s services sector posts growth at 3-month low to 52.5 in February

  • Japan Services PMI revised higher to 52.9 in February amid rise in domestic demand

Other Markets :

In the currency markets, investors responded to signals from central banks, including the Federal Reserve, European Central Bank (ECB), and the Bank of Japan (BOJ), resulting in appreciation for the yen, euro, and GBP against the dollar. Throughout the week, the euro gained 0.92% and the GBP strengthened by 1.63%, contributing to a 1.08% decline in the dollar.


Market sentiment remained cautious amid concerns about Chinese demand, despite the OPEC+ group’s decision to extend supply cuts. WTI crude futures experienced a 2.45% loss for the week, settling 1.17% lower at $78.01 per barrel on Friday. Tight supplies, driven by OPEC’s production cuts and sanctions impacting Russian exports, were overshadowed by worries about reduced demand from China. Chinese oil imports fell by approximately 5.7% in the first two months of the year compared to December figures.


Amid economic uncertainty, safe-haven assets, including gold, continued to climb, posting a 4.57% gain for the week. Gold surged to a new record high, approaching $2,200 an ounce on Friday, buoyed by the latest jobs report reinforcing expectations of imminent interest rate cuts, potentially as early as March.
