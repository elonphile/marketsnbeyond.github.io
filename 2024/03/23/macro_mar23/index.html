Markets Recap - Stocks And Bond Yield Up Except China, BoJ End Its Negative Policy

Summary

  • The US bond markets saw a decline in longer-term Treasury yields following the Federal Reserve’s confirmation of its interest rate outlook, while the stock markets rallied to new highs driven by optimism surrounding Fed policy and advancements in artificial intelligence technology.

  • Similar as US, EU stock markets surged higher this week and bond yields declined on the back of positive economic data and dovish signals from central banks. Asian markets saw Japanese shares hitting all-time highs driven by yen weakness and hawkish Bank of Japan signals, while Chinese equities underperformed due to concerns over the property sector slump despite upbeat economic data.

  • The dollar strengthened this week on expectations of potential interest rate cuts by the Federal Reserve in 2024, while oil prices unchanged amidst hopes for a Gaza ceasefire, and gold edged up amid geopolitical tensions.


US Market :

The US stock markets rallied this week, propelling both the S&P 500 Index and the Nasdaq Composite to new record highs. Investor sentiment was bolstered by reports indicating that Federal Reserve policymakers maintained their expectation of three interest rate cuts later in the year. Notably, optimism surrounding artificial intelligence (AI) further buoyed the markets, with AI chipmaker NVIDIA reaching a record high and nearing a market capitalization of USD 2.4 trillion. Additionally, reports of a potential partnership between Apple and Google parent Alphabet to offer generative AI tools contributed to positive sentiment.


In the US bond markets, the news from the Federal Reserve led to a decline in longer-term Treasury yields over the course of the week. The Federal Reserve’s policy meeting, which concluded on Wednesday, was a key driver of market sentiment. The dot plot released during the meeting revealed that the median expectation for three rate cuts in 2024 remained unchanged and moved toward slowing the pace of reducing their bond holdings, suggesting they aren’t alarmed by a recent uptick in inflation, while projections for interest rates in 2025 and 2026 saw minor adjustments.


Economic Data

  • February’s existing home sales, reported on Thursday, exceeded expectations by jumping 9.5%, providing a positive indicator for the housing market.

  • A gauge of current manufacturing in the Mid-Atlantic region, while slightly lower than February’s reading, surprised investors by indicating a second consecutive month of expansion.

  • Prices paid by businesses in the region fell to their lowest level since May 2020, presenting encouraging signs for inflation dynamics.

EU Market :

The European stock markets closed higher this week, supported by positive economic data and signals from national central banks. Notably, the UK’s inflation rate decelerated from 4.0% in January to 3.4% in February, signaling some moderation in price pressures. Additionally, business activity in the eurozone showed signs of improvement, with the eurozone composite Purchasing Managers’ Index (PMI) reaching a nine-month high of 49.9, up from 49.2 in February. The pan-European STOXX Europe 600 Index ended the week near a record high, posting a gain of 0.96%. Dovish signals from central banks contributed to a risk-on sentiment among investors. Specifically, Germany’s DAX surged 1.35%, Italy’s FTSE MIB advanced 0.19%, and the UK’s FTSE 100 saw a notable increase of 2.63%.


In the bond markets, European government bond yields experienced declines becuase of signals from national banks. Weakness in the German purchasing managers’ survey and a reduction in Swiss interest rates contributed to the downward pressure on bond yields. Furthermore, in the UK, bond yields fell following a dovish tone from the Bank of England (BoE) at its policy meeting.


Economic Data

  • UK retail sales drop 0.40% in February

  • Swiss National Bank surprises with a rate cut, first in developed countries

  • Germany’s Flash Composite PMI touches 3-month high

  • France Flash Composite PMI drops to 47.7 in March

  • UK’s inflation drops to 3.4% in February

  • Euro Area economic sentiment rises to 33.50 points in March

  • Euro Area inflation drops to 2.6% in February, records trade surplus of €11.4B

  • France inflation drops to 3% in February

  • The eurozone’s monthly trade surplus rose to a record high at the start of the year thanks to a sharp drop in the price of energy imports and an uptick in exports

Asian Market :

Japanese shares surged to all-time highs this week, fueled by accelerated inflation in February and primarily driven by yen weakness resulting from the Bank of Japan’s unexpectedly hawkish tilt. Sentiment was further bolstered by expectations of potential interest rate cuts by the U.S. Federal Reserve in 2024, given prevailing inflationary conditions and positive economic growth prospects. The Nikkei 225 Index recorded a notable gain of 5.09%. In the bond markets, Japanese government bond yields moved lower following the Bank of Japan’s much-anticipated policy shift, which saw the central bank exit its negative interest rate policy. The announcement of a policy rate target of 0 to 0.1%, up from -0.1%, coincided with reports of major companies agreeing to robust pay increases in annual wage talks from the previous week.


Chinese equities faced headwinds this week, underperforming other major indices, amidst concerns about the property sector slump despite better-than-expected economic data. The Shanghai Composite Index declined by 0.22%, while the blue-chip CSI 300 retreated by 0.70%. Despite Beijing’s rollout of pro-growth measures to address the prolonged real estate downturn, investor caution prevailed due to lingering issues such as high debt levels among developers and weak demand from homebuyers.


Economic Data :

  • Japan’s trade deficit shrinks more than expected amid robust U.S., China demand

  • Japan’s manufacturing activity rises to 48.2 in March; services PMI increased to 54.9

  • Japan’s industrial output falls 6.7% in January, less than an initial estimate

  • China’s Jan-Feb retail sales rose 5.5%, industrial production up 7.0% exceeds forecasts

  • China’s new home prices drop by 1.4% Y/Y in February

Other Markets :

The dollar strengthened this week amidst expectations of interest rate cuts by the Federal Reserve in June, while the Bank of Japan (BoJ) ended its negative interest rate policy. The yen dipped below 151.42 against the USD, retreating from recent six-week highs as investor sentiment shifted.


Oil prices remained unchanged this week as the possibility of a ceasefire in Gaza and potential impact from the bombardment of Ukraine on Russian’s refineries, which could offset supply concerns, weighed on market sentiment. Reports indicated that the US is preparing to bring a draft UN resolution calling for an immediate and sustained ceasefire on Gaza to a vote in the Security Council. Additionally, EIA data revealed a decrease in gasoline product supplies in the US, indicating potentially weaker consumption and further contributing to the decline in oil prices.


Gold held steady near $2,165 an ounce on Friday, with analysts attributing the rally to various factors, including geopolitical tensions that may have prompted investors to seek refuge in the safe-haven asset.
