Commodity Recap - Navigating Dollar Strength and Unique Drivers

Summary

  • Commodities experienced a broad impact from a stronger dollar or oversupply, with gold, silver, copper, zinc, nickel, tin, and soybean prices all affected.

  • Aluminum, however, deviated from this trend, as its retreat was attributed to strong investments from the UAE and PBOC support, showcasing a unique set of influencing factors. Meanwhile, wheat rebounded on new purchases, and Malaysian palm oil retreated due to reduced inventories from weather-related constraints, introducing specific circumstances for these commodities.


Precious Metals :


Gold & silver price slips. The strength of the US dollar had a ripple effect on other asset classes. Gold prices slipped as the dollar strengthened. Silver prices dips below $23 per ounce. In the Eurozone, inflation increased to 2.9% in December from November’s 2.4%, slightly below the anticipated 3%.

### Base Metals :


Copper & Zinc prices dip on dollar strength and weak China PMI data. Copper futures experienced a decline to 3.9 per pound in January, stepping back from the five-month peak of $3.95 recorded on December 27th. This retreat was influenced by a resurgence in the US dollar, which exerted downward pressure on commodity prices. Additionally, profit-taking activities by commodity trading firms contributed to the dip in copper futures. The subdued demand from China further exacerbated the situation, marked by a fourth consecutive month of low Purchasing Managers’ Index (PMI) readings according to Caixin data. These factors collectively contributed to the weakening of copper prices in the market.


Aluminum retreats on supply enhancement, investments, and PBOC support. Aluminum futures dropped below 2300 from the eight-month high of 2387 last week in 2023. The earlier surge was due to a bauxite supply disruption from Guinea, the world’s third-largest bauxite exporter. An explosion at a fuel depot in Guinea halted industrial activity, driving aluminum prices up. The recent retreat can be attributed to strong investments from the UAE in the infrastructure sector, alleviating investor concerns. Additionally, the People’s Bank of China’s (PBOC) policy to inject 350 billion into state banks is expected to bolster manufacturing activity, providing short-term support to aluminum prices.


Nickel futures dip on oversupply concerns. Nickel futures fell below $16,500 per tonne, nearing three-year lows, due to abundant supply from top producers—Indonesia, Philippines, and China. The International Nickel Study Group forecasts a surplus of 223,000 metric tons in 2023, with expectations of widening to 239,000 metric tons in 2024, emphasizing the persistent imbalance between supply and demand in the nickel market.


Tin dips on dollar strength, easing supply. Tin futures experienced a dip in the first week, influenced by a strengthening dollar and easing supply concerns from the previous year, following a three-month high of $25,680 reached on December 28th. Despite the decline, tin prices were upheld by the prolonged production halt in Myanmar’s Wa region since August, responsible for 70% of Myanmar’s output and a significant portion of China’s tin supply. With China compelled to seek alternative sources, the Republic of Congo became a primary supplier. However, adverse weather conditions and infrastructure challenges in Congo towards the end of the year hampered output, contributing to elevated tin prices in the market.


Lithium remained unchanged this week in such restocking season. Lithium carbonate prices, hitting CNY 97,500 per tonne in January, marked the lowest since August 2021, plummeting over 80% in the past year due to an escalating oversupply. Pessimism surrounding electric vehicle sales in China constrained lithium demand during the typical restocking season for battery manufacturers. Consequently, major industry players now project the next lithium deficit to emerge only in 2028, a sharp departure from earlier expectations. Previous investments aimed at increasing supply may propel the global carbonate equivalent supply to surge by 40% in 2024, as per UBS, intensifying the existing surplus.

Agriculture :


Soybean futures drop on oversupply. Soybean futures dropped to $12.6 per bushel in the first week, marking the lowest point in almost three months and extending a 15.1% loss from 2023 due to anticipated robust production. Despite weather challenges, Brazil is poised for a record-breaking soybean harvest. Additionally, Argentina is exploring the potential to double its soybean output, while Paraguay and Uruguay also indicate substantial increases.


Wheat futures rebound a little bit on buyer’s purchases. Wheat futures climbed above6.2 per bushel, recovering from the one-month low of 6 on January 3rd, following reports of new purchases from major consumers. The announcement that China was seeking another round of US wheat spurred buying activity from commodity funds. This potential purchase builds on a similar transaction at the end of November, which previously boosted the benchmark to $6.4.


Plam oil retreated. Malaysian palm oil futures hovered around MYR 3,700 per tonne as December’s end likely saw a decline in Malaysia’s palm oil inventories. The reduction can be attributed to diminished production caused by rainy weather and seasonal effects, as reported by a Reuters survey.
