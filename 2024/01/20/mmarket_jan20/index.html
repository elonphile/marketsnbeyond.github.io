Regional Markets - Currency Declined As Dollar Strengthed

Summary

  • In the emerging markets, most currencies faced decline due to the strengthened dollar resulting from a hawkish Fed Reserve governor.Mexico’s peso faces pressure on policy outlook, while Brazil grapples with industrial contraction.

  • The Indian rupee held firm amid global uncertainties, benefiting from strong domestic economic data and central bank intervention. In Russia, the ruble weakened after recent highs, prompting forex sales from the finance ministry. Meanwhile, Turkey’s lira hit a new record low, facing economic challenges and anticipating the conclusion of its monetary tightening cycle.


Latin America :

Brazil

Real hits one-month low below 4.92/USD amid dollar strength, Ibovespa impacted by uncertainties over tax exemptions and rising interest rates. The Brazilian real slipped below 4.92 per USD, aligning with global emerging market trends. Risk assets declined as expectations for Fed rate cuts in March diminished, leading to a rise in the U.S. dollar and Treasury yields. Brazil’s Ibovespa edged 0.2% higher but reached an over-one-month low amid uncertainty over tax exemptions and rising interest rates, aggravated by declining iron ore prices.



Mexico

Peso weakens to one-month low beyond 17.15/USD due to a strong dollar, reflecting concerns about record-high interest rates. In Mexico, the peso weakened to its lowest in a month, surpassing 17.15 per USD, under pressure from a stronger dollar as markets assessed the Bank of Mexico’s policy outlook. The stock index faced declines alongside negative industrial production data, showing a 1% contraction in November, the fastest in over two years. The data reflects the impact of record-high interest rates set by Banxico at 11.25%, raising discussions about potential easing in the upcoming quarters.


Argentina

The USD to ARS exchange rate increased by 0.45% on Friday for the week as more harkish stance from US FED RESERVE goveror. Notably, Argentina’s main stock market index, Merval, surged 13.72% this week.

Asia :

India

The Indian rupee edged past 83 per USD, retracting from its four-month high of 82.2 on January 15th, as expectations of delayed Federal Reserve rate cuts boosted the dollar. Despite this, the rupee’s depreciation was comparatively modest due to robust domestic economic data and consistent RBI intervention. The 7.3% GDP growth in the 2023-24 fiscal year surpassed expectations, reinforcing positive sentiments for Indian equities and sovereign debt. Meanwhile, the index faced a 1.2% decline for the week, influenced by global concerns over delayed rate cuts and rising Middle East tensions.

EU :

Russia

Russian currency and index react positively to strengthed dollar and geopolitical tension in Yemen. The Russian ruble eased to approximately 89 per USD as profit-taking ensued following a recent rally, reaching its highest levels in over six months. The Russian finance ministry shifted to forex sales, planning to sell 16.7 billion rubles ($189.5 million) daily in the latter half of January. The ruble-based MOEX Russia index, which had surged to 1-1/2-month highs, declined to 3166 on Friday for a second consecutive session. Mixed dynamics were observed over the week, with all sectors ending in the red amid reduced investor activity and a light macroeconomic agenda.


Turkey

The Turkish lira continued its downward spiral reaching an unprecedented low of 30.2 per USD, prompted by remarks from central bank chief Erkan signaling an early end to the monetary policy tightening cycle and strengthed dollar. Despite Turkey’s annual inflation soaring to an alarming 64.77% in December, with expectations of a continued upward trajectory in the coming months, Erkan’s comments added to concerns. The substantial increase in the minimum wage, a contributing factor to inflation, aimed at alleviating living costs ahead of the upcoming municipal elections in March. With the net minimum wage for 2024 raised to 17,002 liras ($578), the Turkish lira has witnessed a staggering decline of over a third in the past year and more than 80% over the last five years, reflecting the deepening economic challenges facing Turkey.
